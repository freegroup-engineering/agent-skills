# Influence — working skill

Apply Robert Cialdini's research to anything you write that's meant to change a reader's behavior. This is a pre-draft checklist, not a rhetorical garnish to add at the end.

## The decision framework (do this every time)

Before you draft anything persuasive:

**1. Write the one action.** One sentence. "The reader clicks 'Make my video.'" "Kling replies with an intro." "Ronen approves hiring Cleo." If you can't state it in one sentence, stop — you don't know what you're writing.

**2. Write the one real objection.** Not the one you wish they had. The actual reason they won't act. "Parents think AI video for toddlers is creepy." "Kling gets 50 of these asks a week." "Ronen is worried about agent sprawl." Persuasion that doesn't address the real objection is decoration.

**3. Pick 2–3 principles.** Not seven. Seven reads as manipulation. Use the table at the bottom to pick. Each principle you pick must be one you can *earn*, not one you can *fake*.

**4. Pre-suade.** What's the very first thing the reader sees — the subject line, the hero image, the opening line? That's your privileged moment (see `pre-suasion.md`). It sets what they think *about* before they think about your ask. Choose it consciously.

**5. Draft. Then audit.** After drafting, re-read and ask: is every principle earned, or did I fake one? Fake authority, fake scarcity, fake social proof — these are expensive. Princeton's 2019 dark-patterns crawl found ~40% of countdown timers online were fake; research shows manufactured scarcity increases short-term conversion but erodes trust and repeat purchase intent. Short-term win, long-term loss.

## The 7 principles — quick reference

### 1. Reciprocity
People return genuine favors. Give something of real value *first*, then ask.

- **Earn it:** free usable output (not a lead magnet), a thoughtful intro, specific feedback, a tool, something that cost you to make.
- **Fake it (don't):** "free guides" that are 90% pitch, gifts with obvious strings.
- **Canonical research:** Regan 1971 (Coke study) — a small unsolicited favor doubled raffle-ticket compliance, beating mere liking as a predictor. Strong & Lynn 2003 — giving diners a mint with the check lifted tips ~3%; two mints + "and because you've been so nice, here's another" lifted tips ~21%. Delivery matters as much as the gift.
- **Iniminimo move:** generate a free first video *good enough to stand alone*, before asking for signup or payment. Compute cost is real; the gift is real.
- **Outreach move:** lead with value already delivered. "Here's a video we made with Kling's API, here's where we hit the rate ceiling" beats "can we get credits."

### 2. Commitment & Consistency
People stay consistent with prior actions, especially public or written ones.

- **Earn it:** a multi-step flow where each step is a small, genuine yes (Freedman & Fraser 1966 foot-in-the-door: a small sign in the window → 76% later agreed to a huge lawn sign, vs. 17% cold). Ask the user to *articulate their reason* in their own words — written commitment is the strongest form.
- **Fake it (don't):** low-balling (Cialdini, Cacioppo, Bassett & Miller 1978) where the terms change after commitment — works once, destroys trust forever.
- **Iniminimo move:** on the waitlist, ask "what milestone is hardest right now?" Once a parent names it, they've committed to needing a solution. Carry that answer through the onboarding.
- **Watch:** don't engineer commitments the reader didn't really make. Reactance follows.

### 3. Social proof
Under uncertainty, people copy similar others (Milgram, Bickman & Berkowitz 1969 — one person staring up made ~40% of passers-by look; five staring up got ~80%).

- **Earn it:** specific testimonials with real names/photos, concrete numbers ("2,847 parents"), UGC, "people like you" framing. Similarity matters more than celebrity (Cialdini).
- **Fake it (don't):** vague counts, bought reviews, celebrity endorsements from outside the target audience.
- **Uncertainty multiplier:** most powerful when the reader isn't sure what to do. Parents deciding whether an AI video is safe/helpful = uncertain = social proof is load-bearing.
- **Iniminimo move:** video of another toddler reacting to their personalized song. That's proof of the *emotional* payoff, not the product spec.
- **Outreach move:** "Mark and three other YC founders we met at [event] use Kling" > "we're a startup." Real, specific, similar.

### 4. Authority
People defer to credible experts and authority markers. Milgram 1963 — the lab coat and Yale setting drove 65% of subjects to full-shock compliance; swapping to a run-down Bridgeport office dropped it to 48%. Context + credentials matter.

- **Earn it:** pediatrician or child-development advisor, cited research, press with links, specific expertise visibly displayed in the copy itself ("we noticed toddlers under 2.5 don't generalize from cartoon characters to themselves, which is why…").
- **Fake it (don't):** "As seen on" when it was a quote in a listicle; vague "experts agree"; borrowed brand logos without real relationships.
- **Iniminimo move:** a named child-development advisor + one-sentence citation of the developmental reason milestones feel scary to toddlers. The "milestone" framing itself is an authority move — it invokes developmental psychology rather than entertainment.

### 5. Liking
People say yes to people they like. Drivers: similarity, sincere compliments, cooperation toward a shared goal, attractiveness, familiarity (Frenzen & Davis 1990 on Tupperware — liking for the hostess was 2× as predictive of purchase as liking the product).

- **Earn it:** write parent-to-parent, not brand-to-parent. Show you live their daily reality. Share a bit of your own story (Ronen + Eve building this for Liev — use it, it's load-bearing).
- **Fake it (don't):** generic flattery, "fellow founder" openers on cold emails, corporate voice pretending to be casual.
- **Outreach move:** tone > content. Warm, specific, humble, one sentence of genuine shared context beats any polished ask.

### 6. Scarcity
People want what's scarce, disappearing, or recently lost. Worchel, Lee & Adewole 1975 cookie jar study: cookies in a 2-jar were rated more valuable than the same cookies in a 10-jar; cookies that *went from abundant to scarce* were rated highest — loss is more motivating than absence.

- **Earn it:** real limits (500-person beta, founding pricing that genuinely ends, limited-theme drops tied to a real constraint).
- **Fake it (don't):** resetting countdowns, "only 3 left" when there are thousands, invented urgency. Parents are marketing-literate — they will clock this and leave.
- **Iniminimo move:** "founding parents" cohort with real, permanent benefits (locked price, vote on new themes, direct line to the team). Not a fake timer on the landing page.

### 7. Unity
Shared identity — not just similar, but "we are the same kind of person." Family, tribe, team. Added by Cialdini in the 2016 "Influence: New and Expanded" / Pre-Suasion. Asking for *advice* (not feedback) puts the reader in a collaborative frame; invoking family/tribe membership drives compliance dramatically.

- **Earn it:** name the tribe the reader wants to belong to. "Parents who think toddlers deserve to feel seen." "Early-stage founders building with AI video." Ask for *advice* when you want alignment; ask for *opinion* when you want distance.
- **Fake it (don't):** hollow "community" that's a newsletter; "join the movement" with no movement.
- **Iniminimo move:** there is a real tribe — exhausted parents fed up with generic kid content who want something emotionally attuned to their specific child. Name it. Make them recognize themselves.
- **This is often the strongest lever for consumer products where identity matters.**

## Pre-suasion (the before-the-ask move)

Cialdini's 2016 follow-up argues the *moment before* the message matters as much as the message. Whatever you draw attention to first gets over-weighted in the decision.

- Subject line / hero image / opening sentence = your privileged moment.
- If you want parents to feel their kid deserves something custom, open with a line that makes them picture *their* kid, not your product.
- If you want a partner to say yes to a credits ask, open with *their* work, specifically. Not yours.
- Ask a question the reader wants the answer to — their attention is now yours voluntarily (no reactance).

See `pre-suasion.md` for more.

## Common failure modes (full list in `failure-modes.md`)

- **Leaning on all 7.** Reads as manipulation. Pick 2–3.
- **Fake versions.** Fake social proof, borrowed authority, manufactured scarcity — all cash out as trust debt. For a pre-revenue parenting product, trust is the asset. Don't spend it.
- **Principle without clear action.** The reader feels the pull but doesn't know what to do. Every message ends with a clear, frictionless next step.
- **Wrong objection.** You addressed the objection you wished they had. Re-do step 2.
- **Persuasion where trust is the bottleneck.** For high-stakes parental decisions (anything involving their kid), trust-building (transparency, showing the math, slowing down) beats persuasion. The Persuasion Knowledge Model (Friestad & Wright 1994): once the reader detects you're trying to persuade them, they discount. Don't trigger it.
- **Reactance.** Brehm 1966 — when freedom feels threatened, people reassert it by doing the opposite. High-pressure urgency, guilt, and "you must" framings trigger it. Softer, opt-in framings don't.

## Ethical floor (non-negotiable)

Don't use these to:
- Get people to do things not in their interest.
- Exploit fear, loneliness, or parental guilt beyond what the product honestly addresses.
- Create commitments the reader didn't understand they were making.
- Manufacture urgency, scarcity, authority, or social proof that isn't real.

**Test:** would you be embarrassed to explain the technique to the person you're persuading? If yes, don't use it. Cialdini himself draws this line as the difference between *influence* (ethical, based on true information) and *manipulation* (based on fabricated cues).

## Which principle for which situation

| Situation | Lean on |
| --- | --- |
| New product, audience uncertain | Social proof + authority |
| High-price or high-commitment purchase | Reciprocity + commitment + unity |
| Short-term promo / conversion push | Scarcity (real) + social proof |
| B2B cold outreach | Liking + reciprocity + authority |
| Asking for a favor (credits, intro, access) | Reciprocity + liking + unity |
| Community / waitlist building | Unity + commitment + scarcity |
| Retention / re-engagement | Commitment (remind them of their stated goal) + liking |
| Referral loop | Unity + reciprocity + social proof |
| Onboarding flow | Commitment (small yeses) + social proof |
| Pricing page | Authority + scarcity (real) + social proof |
| Parenting-vertical copy | Liking (parent-to-parent) + authority (developmental science) + unity (the tribe) |
| Negotiation for better terms | Reciprocity (concessions) + commitment escalation |

## Digital channel notes

- **Landing page:** hero = pre-suasion + one principle. Sub-hero = second principle. Social proof strip below fold. Don't cram.
- **Email:** subject line = pre-suasion. Opening = liking. One ask. One CTA. Under 100 words for cold, under 200 for warm.
- **SMS:** one principle max. Usually commitment ("you said you wanted X — here's X") or scarcity (real only).
- **Ad copy:** unity + one emotional payoff. Text-only channels strip out warmth cues, so liking/unity has to be explicit in word choice.
- **Product UI:** commitment (progress bars, saved state), social proof (activity feeds, "3 parents made a video in the last hour" if true), authority (citations in empty states).
- **Mobile:** attention is scarcer, pre-suasion matters more — the first visible element does most of the work.

```
1. Action (1 sentence): __________
2. Real objection: __________

5. Draft
6. Audit: earned or faked? any reactance triggers? clear CTA?
```
