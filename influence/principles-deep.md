# The 7 principles — deep reference

Research-level notes on each of Cialdini's principles. Open this when `SKILL.md` isn't enough and you need to understand *why* something will or won't work.

---

## 1. Reciprocity

**Core mechanism.** The norm of reciprocity is cross-cultural and near-universal (Gouldner 1960). People experience an obligation to return favors, and the obligation is disproportionate to the size of the initial favor. It's also *uninvited* — the recipient didn't have to ask, which eliminates the usual "do I want this" filter.

**Canonical studies.**
- **Regan (1971)** — The Coke experiment, Cornell. Pairs rated paintings; a confederate ("Joe") either bought the subject an unrequested Coke or didn't. Later Joe asked subjects to buy raffle tickets. Subjects given a Coke bought twice as many tickets. Liking for Joe predicted ticket purchases in the control condition — but in the Coke condition, liking was irrelevant. The favor alone drove compliance.
- **Strohmetz, Rind, Fisher & Lynn (2002)** — restaurant tipping. Giving a single mint with the check increased tips ~3%. Two mints increased tips ~14%. One mint, turning back, and giving a second "because you've been so nice" produced ~21% tip lifts. The *personalization and surprise* of the reciprocal act mattered as much as its size.
- **Cialdini et al. (1975)** — door-in-the-face / reciprocal concessions. First request is huge and rejected; second, smaller request is accepted at much higher rates than if offered cold. The "concession" from the asker is perceived as a gift requiring reciprocation.

**Sub-patterns.**
- **Uninvited gift** — strongest form. Asking "can I help?" weakens the effect.
- **Reciprocal concession / door-in-the-face** — start high, step down.
- **Trust-building reciprocity** — repeated small gifts over time.
- **Information reciprocity** — sharing vulnerable info invites the reader to share back (foundational to warm outreach).

**Boundary conditions / when it fails.**
- Gifts that obviously cost the giver nothing (spam lead magnets) don't trigger it.
- Gifts with visible strings attached activate persuasion knowledge and kill it (Friestad & Wright 1994).
- Some cultural and individual variation in how strongly the debt is felt; highly individualist readers may actively resist feeling indebted.

**So what.** If you want reciprocity to do real work, the gift must be (a) real value to *them* (not a thin disguise for the ask), (b) ideally personalized, (c) given before any ask is visible. For iniminimo: a free usable first video. For outreach: something you already did, not something you'd do if they said yes.

## 2. Commitment & Consistency

**Core mechanism.** People are motivated to behave consistently with their prior actions, stated beliefs, and self-image (Festinger 1957 — cognitive dissonance lineage). Commitments that are *active, public, written, and freely chosen* have much more pull than passive, private, or coerced ones.

**Canonical studies.**
- **Freedman & Fraser (1966)** — foot-in-the-door. Homeowners were asked to put a small "Be a safe driver" sign or sign a petition. Two weeks later, a second request: put a huge ugly "Drive Carefully" sign on the lawn. Compliance with the big request: 17% cold, 76% after the small prior yes.
- **Cialdini, Cacioppo, Bassett & Miller (1978)** — low-balling. Subjects agreed to a study at a normal hour, then were told it actually required 7 a.m. attendance. Low-ball group show-up rate: 53%; cold-ask rate: 24%. Commitment held even when the terms changed.
- **Deutsch & Gerard (1955)** — public written commitments were the hardest to reverse; private unwritten commitments were easiest.

**Sub-patterns.**
- **Foot-in-the-door** — small yes → bigger yes.
- **Low-ball** — commit, then change terms. Ethically dicey. Cialdini explicitly flags this.
- **Public commitment** — stated in front of others.
- **Written commitment** — stronger than verbal.
- **Effortful commitment** — the more they invested, the more they justify continuing (IKEA effect, Norton, Mochon & Ariely 2012).
- **Labeling** — tell someone they're "the kind of person who X" and they act more like that person.

**Boundary conditions / when it fails.**
- If the initial commitment was obviously coerced or trivial, the effect disappears.
- Guadagno, Asher, Demaine & Cialdini (2001) — people high in "preference for inconsistency" can show reverse foot-in-the-door effects.
- When the second ask is drastically larger and obviously unrelated, the chain breaks.
- Repeated low-balls destroy the relationship.

**So what.** Design your flow so the *earliest* interaction is a meaningful-but-small commitment tied to the identity you want the reader to adopt. For iniminimo onboarding: ask the parent to name the milestone before anything else — now they've committed to a problem the product solves. Don't ever change the terms after commitment; the short-term win is dwarfed by trust loss.

## 3. Social Proof

**Core mechanism.** Under uncertainty, people treat others' behavior as information about correct behavior ("informational social influence," Deutsch & Gerard 1955). Similarity amplifies this — we're most swayed by people like us. Cialdini frames this as: the less we know and the more similar they are, the more we copy.

**Canonical studies.**
- **Milgram, Bickman & Berkowitz (1969)** — NYC sidewalk study. One person staring up at a building: ~40% of passers-by looked up. Five people: ~80%. Fifteen: saturates.
- **Cialdini, Reno & Kallgren (1990)** — littering norms. People littered less in clean environments than dirty ones, not because of rules but because clean environments cued descriptive norms of non-littering.
- **Goldstein, Cialdini & Griskevicius (2008)** — hotel towel reuse. "75% of guests in this room reused their towels" outperformed generic environmental appeals. Specific, similar, descriptive norm beats abstract injunctive norm.
- **Salganik, Dodds & Watts (2006)** — MusicLab experiment. In a world where download counts were visible, outcomes became wildly path-dependent on early random variation; in the invisible world, intrinsic quality dominated. Social proof doesn't just reflect quality, it creates winners.

**Sub-patterns.**
- **Descriptive norms** — what most people *do*. Usually stronger than injunctive.
- **Injunctive norms** — what most people *approve of*.
- **Wisdom of the crowd** — many similar strangers.
- **Wisdom of friends** — few highly similar people.
- **UGC / testimonials / reviews** — specificity and photos beat vague counts.
- **Live social proof** — "12 people are viewing this right now" (only effective if true).

**Boundary conditions / when it fails.**
- When the reader feels certain about their preference, social proof has minimal effect.
- When proof comes from dissimilar others, it can *reduce* compliance (reverse identification).
- Fake social proof, once detected, triggers strong persuasion-knowledge discounting.
- Negative social proof backfires: "Most people don't recycle" telegraphs that non-recycling is the norm and reduces recycling.

**So what.** Social proof is load-bearing for new, uncertain, or emotionally risky decisions — exactly where iniminimo lives (AI video for your 2-year-old). Use *specific, similar* parents. Real names, real photos, the kid's name, a sentence in the parent's own voice. Not "thousands of happy families."

## 4. Authority

**Core mechanism.** People defer to credible experts and to cues of authority (titles, credentials, institutional affiliation, uniforms, settings) — often well past the point of reason. Cialdini: the brain treats authority as a shortcut that usually pays off.

**Canonical studies.**
- **Milgram (1963)** — obedience study. With a Yale lab coat and experimenter, 65% delivered the full 450V shock. Moving the lab from Yale to a run-down Bridgeport office dropped it to 48%. Removing the lab coat and replacing the experimenter with a "regular person" dropped it to ~20%. Setting + uniform + title all mattered.
- **Bickman (1974)** — confederates on the street asked passers-by to pick up litter or give a dime. In a security uniform: compliance ~90%. In street clothes: ~40%.
- **Hofling et al. (1966)** — nurses followed telephoned instructions from an unknown "doctor" to administer a clearly excessive drug dose in 21 of 22 trials, despite hospital policy.

**Sub-patterns.**
- **Titles** — "Dr.", "Prof.", "CEO".
- **Clothing / visible credentials** — lab coat, uniform.
- **Trappings** — expensive office, institutional logos, letterheads.
- **Expert testimony** — citing named experts in your copy.
- **Demonstrated expertise** — showing deep knowledge in the copy itself.
- **Admitting a minor weakness** — paradoxically increases authority (the Cialdini "flaw admission" — when an ad admits a small downside, credibility on everything else rises).

**Boundary conditions / when it fails.**
- Borrowed/fake authority ("As seen in Forbes" when it was a contributor-post mention) is a fuse — once lit, trust goes.
- Authority cues from outside the reader's frame fail ("endorsed by a celebrity you've never heard of").
- Self-declared authority without evidence reads as compensatory.
- Authority is less effective for emotional/identity decisions than for technical ones.

**So what.** For iniminimo, the load-bearing authority is *developmental psychology*. Cite it, name an advisor, show you understand it in the copy itself. For outreach (Kling, MiniMax): authority = showing what you've built. Don't claim authority; *demonstrate* it in one sentence of genuine domain insight.

## 5. Liking

**Core mechanism.** People say yes to those they like. Drivers per Cialdini:
- **Physical attractiveness** — halo effect (Dion, Berscheid & Walster 1972).
- **Similarity** — opinion, background, dress, lifestyle.
- **Compliments** — even when obviously strategic, they still increase liking (Drachman, deCarufel & Insko 1978).
- **Familiarity / contact** — mere exposure (Zajonc 1968).
- **Cooperation toward shared goals** — especially under superordinate-goal framing (Sherif 1961 Robbers Cave).
- **Conditioning & association** — pairing self with positives (weather, good news, attractive models) and avoiding being associated with negatives.

**Canonical studies.**
- **Frenzen & Davis (1990)** — Tupperware parties. Guests' liking for the *hostess* was twice as predictive of purchase as their liking for the product itself.
- **Emswiller, Deaux & Willits (1971)** — confederates dressed similarly to subjects got change for a phone call ~2× as often as dissimilar confederates.
- **Howard (1990)** — cold callers who opened with "how are you feeling this evening?" and got a positive response had 2× the compliance rate vs. a no-preamble opener.

**Sub-patterns.**
- **"Just like you" positioning** — voice, vocabulary, references that match the reader's world.
- **Self-disclosure** — share something first to prompt reciprocal liking.
- **Named, specific praise** — for what the person actually did.
- **Founder-as-user story** — "we built this because we needed it" is a liking anchor.
- **Warmth signals** — first-person plural, informal contractions, acknowledging the reader's state.

**Boundary conditions / when it fails.**
- Generic flattery reads as manipulation (but still works somewhat — Drachman et al.).
- Over-familiarity too fast (cold email pretending to be an old friend) triggers reactance.
- Liking transfers less well through text than through voice/video.
- In negotiation, excessive liking can make the counterparty distrust the tactic.

**So what.** For iniminimo, Ronen + Eve + Liev is the liking engine. Use real names, a real photo, one real detail. For outreach to Kling/MiniMax, open with a specific, concrete, genuine observation of *their* work before pivoting to your ask — similarity + sincere compliment in one move.

## 6. Scarcity

**Core mechanism.** Scarce = valuable. Also: freedoms/opportunities that are disappearing feel more valuable than ones that are merely absent (reactance, Brehm 1966). People are loss-averse (Kahneman & Tversky 1979), so "you will lose X if you don't act" outperforms "you can gain X if you act" for many behaviors.

**Canonical studies.**
- **Worchel, Lee & Adewole (1975)** — the cookie jar study. Same cookies were rated higher when the jar held 2 vs. 10. When subjects first saw a 10-cookie jar and 8 were removed ("now scarce"), ratings were highest of all. "Went from abundant to scarce" > "always scarce" > "always abundant."
- **Inman, Peter & Raghubir (1997)** — limited-quantity promotions outperformed equivalent limited-time promotions in field tests; both outperformed unrestricted ones.
- **Mittone & Savadori (2009)** — scarcity increases desirability even for items subjects have no prior interest in, demonstrating the heuristic operates cue-to-response.

**Sub-patterns.**
- **Quantity scarcity** — only N units.
- **Time scarcity** — ends on date X.
- **Access scarcity** — only members get it.
- **Uniqueness scarcity** — personalized / one-of-one (iniminimo's product is inherently this).
- **Competition scarcity** — multiple bidders for the same thing.
- **Disappearing-freedom framing** — "we're closing this to new signups after launch" more motivating than "sign up now."

**Boundary conditions / when it fails.**
- Detection of fakery is devastating. Princeton's 2019 dark-patterns crawl found ~40% of countdown timers on sampled e-commerce sites were fake; consumer research links manufactured scarcity to measurable declines in repeat-purchase intent.
- Scarcity on commodities that obviously aren't scarce reads as condescension.
- In low-uncertainty situations (reader knows the item well), scarcity is weaker than social proof.
- Habitual use of scarcity by a brand decays its effect (like any alarm).

**So what.** Use scarcity only when it's real and structurally enforced. For iniminimo, the cleanest use is a founding-parents cohort with *real* locked-in benefits — not a fake timer. For outreach, scarcity appears subtly: "we're picking two launch partners this month" only if true.

## 7. Unity

**Core mechanism.** Beyond mere similarity, *shared identity*: "we are the same kind of person / family / tribe." Cialdini added this as the 7th principle in "Influence: New and Expanded" (2016) and in Pre-Suasion. Unity predicts behavior more strongly than liking because it triggers the self as the motivator — helping "us" is helping "me."

**Canonical studies and examples.**
- **Burger, Messian, Patel, del Prado & Anderson (2004)** — "incidental similarities" (shared birthday, shared first name, shared fingerprint type) substantially increased compliance with a request, even when the similarity was arbitrary.
- **Advice-vs-feedback studies** (summarized in Pre-Suasion) — asking for "advice" puts the reader in a merged-self / collaborative frame; asking for "opinion" or "feedback" puts them in a distanced-evaluator frame. The advice frame produces more positive responses and commitment.
- **Kin / family framing** — requests framed around helping a family member see response-rate multipliers (the "help my nephew" effect Cialdini describes).
- **In-group identification** — readers act more pro-socially toward identified in-group members, across many lab and field contexts (Tajfel social identity tradition).

**Sub-patterns.**
- **Naming the tribe** — "parents who believe…", "builders who…", "the kind of person who…"
- **Co-creation framing** — "help us decide X" pulls the reader inside the tent.
- **Asking for advice** — stronger than asking for opinion.
- **Founder-origin / shared-struggle framing** — "we're parents too" / "we're founders too."
- **Shared ritual / language** — internal jokes, shared references signal tribe membership.

**Boundary conditions / when it fails.**
- Hollow "community" (it's really just a newsletter) triggers reactance fast.
- Claiming membership you haven't earned ("fellow parents" when you're not) is instant trust damage.
- Over-broad tribe names ("entrepreneurs") don't produce identification; specific and emotional tribe names do.

**So what.** Unity is often the strongest principle for identity-heavy consumer products. For iniminimo: the tribe is *specifically* parents who are tired of generic kids' content and want emotionally attuned media for their specific child. For outreach to other founders: shared-builder frame, ask for advice (not a favor) when possible.

## Cross-principle interactions

- **Reciprocity + commitment** — give a gift, then ask for a small commitment. Pairs well.
- **Social proof + similarity (liking)** — specific similar others > generic many.
- **Authority + liking** — warm expert > cold expert.
- **Unity + commitment** — once tribe membership is salient, small commitments consistent with the tribe's identity land harder.
- **Scarcity + social proof** — "500 other parents already claimed one" — but only if true.

Don't chain more than 2–3 principles in the same message — detection threshold rises sharply.
