# Negotiation & outreach playbook

For cold email, founder-to-founder outreach, partner asks (API credits, access, intros), and negotiation. Freddie uses this more than Mark; Mark uses it for BD and partnership asks.

## Core idea

Most cold asks fail because the sender leads with the ask. The highest-response cold messages lead with *something already done for the reader or their world* — reciprocity and/or pre-suasion — and defer the ask to the second-to-last sentence.

## The structure that works

A short cold ask (under 100 words) should do all five of these in this order:

1. **Specific observation about them** (pre-suasion + liking). One sentence. Not flattery — a concrete detail proving you actually looked.
2. **Something you've already done** (reciprocity). Built with their API, shared their work, made an intro, wrote a note. Real, not promised.
3. **Who you are in one honest line** (authority + liking). No puffery. What you build + who you build it with.
4. **The ask, specific, small enough to say yes to** (commitment — small first yes).
5. **Opt-out easier than opt-in** (reactance reduction). "If it's not a fit, no need to reply" outperforms "let me know either way."

## Principles mapped to outreach moves

- **Reciprocity** — lead with value already delivered. "We made this with your API, here's the result and where we hit the ceiling." Not "if you give us credits we'll build something cool."
- **Commitment** — start with a low-friction ask (a yes/no, a 3-line reply, "are you the right person?") rather than a 30-minute call. Research on cold email reply rates: low-friction asks get ~3× the response of high-friction asks.
- **Social proof** — real, specific, similar. "Nova and two other builders we met through [mutual context] use your API." Not "we're a fast-growing startup."
- **Authority** — a single sentence of genuine domain insight demonstrates authority far more than credentials. Show don't tell.
- **Liking** — write in their register. If they write playful, be playful. If they write technical, be technical. The mismatch signals you didn't look.
- **Scarcity** — don't manufacture it. Real scarcity ("we're picking two launch partners this month and I think you'd be the strongest fit") only if actually true.
- **Unity** — ask for *advice*, not a favor, when possible. "We're hitting a tricky decision — can I get your advice?" invokes the merged-self / collaborator frame. Much higher response.

## Specific tactics

### Cold email (first touch)
- Under 100 words.
- Subject line: specific and human. Not "Quick question" or "partnership opportunity." Try the concrete observation or an honest descriptor of the ask.
- Open with *them*, not you. Second sentence can introduce you, briefly.
- One ask. One CTA. One clear yes/no.
- Avoid "quick question" (everyone uses it), "I hope this finds you well" (dead signal), "I'll keep this brief" (says you can't).

### Cold email (follow-up)
- Short. Reference the original. Add one new thing.
- Don't guilt. "Figured I'd bump this in case it slipped" is fine. "Wanted to make sure you didn't miss it" is passive-aggressive.
- Most responses come after touch 2–4. Don't stop at 1.

### Asking for API credits / technical access (Kling, MiniMax, etc.)
- Lead with *what you've built* with their product. Include a link to output. This is load-bearing reciprocity (compute cost is real).
- Be specific about where the product ceiling is hurting you. They want to know the pain.
- Offer something specific in return: feedback on their API, a case study, inclusion in launch materials, a quote, UGC. Make it concrete.
- Ask for a specific amount, not "credits" in the abstract.
- Name a person where possible (founder, DevRel, ecosystem lead). The closer to the actual decision-maker, the better.

### Partner negotiation
- **Reciprocal concessions.** Start your counteroffer higher than you need, concede in visible steps. The *act of conceding* triggers the reciprocity norm even if your endpoint was your target all along.
- **Commitment escalation.** Build a chain of smaller yeses leading to the big one. "Does this framing make sense to you?" → "Would you be open to a pilot?" → "Would a pilot of X duration work?" Each yes makes the next easier.
- **Labeling.** "You strike me as someone who cares about long-term partners over short-term wins" — if genuine — pulls the counterparty toward acting consistently with that label.
- **Anchors.** Whoever names the first number moves the conversation toward it. In partnership talks, anchor *your* way. In credits asks, anchor ambitiously but not insultingly.

### Internal comms (Freddie ↔ Ronen; cross-agent)
- **Unity via shared context.** "Given what we're trying to do this quarter, X seems to pull against Y — worth your call" pulls Ronen into the frame as decision-maker, not gatekeeper.
- **Pre-suasion via framing.** The first line of your message sets what Ronen thinks about before your ask. If you want him evaluating costs, put the cost frame first. If you want him evaluating speed, open with the deadline.
- **Commitment.** When Ronen has stated a priority, reference it verbatim before proposing aligned work. Don't reframe his words.
- **Low-friction asks.** "Approve or redirect?" is a yes/no. "What do you think of this approach?" is a request for thought-work and will sit in his queue. Use the former when you can.

## Short case-study patterns (generic, for illustration)

- **Lenny Rachitsky's cold-email success story** (widely discussed): a single concrete observation about the recipient's product + an offer to share a specific user insight. No ask in the first email; ask emerged naturally in the reply thread.
- **YC-style partnership ask:** "We're [one line]. We built [specific thing] using your API — here's a 15-second demo [link]. We're running into [specific technical ceiling]. Would [specific amount of credits / access] be possible for [specific scoped use]? Happy to share a case study either way." 60-second read. Works.
- **Negotiating concessions:** Chris Voss's "mirror + label" techniques (from *Never Split the Difference*) complement Cialdini — mirror their last three words, label the emotion, then ask a calibrated open question ("how am I supposed to do that?"). Pairs well with reciprocal-concession framing.

## What to avoid

- Templated personalization that's obviously a mail-merge ({{FirstName}}, "I saw your work on {{Company}}").
- "I'll keep this brief" (doesn't — and signals you're not in control of the email).
- Fake urgency ("responding today only").
- Claiming mutual connections you don't have.
- Asking for too much too soon. Your first ask should be well below the lifetime ask.
- Emotional asks ("this could change our company") without a concrete value offer. You're asking for a gift; asking for gifts is fine if you acknowledge it's a gift, but don't dress it as a deal.

## Ethical floor

Don't fabricate the observation, the connection, the prior work, the social proof, or the scarcity. It *works in the moment* and it *fails cumulatively* — your name starts getting forwarded as "be careful about this person." For founders, trust-debt compounds faster than trust-credit.

## Quick template (adapt — don't ship as-is)

```
Subject: [concrete observation — not "quick question"]

Hi [Name],

[One specific sentence about them — proof you looked. Ideally something other than "I saw your post."]

We're [one line, honest, concrete]. I've been using [their product/API] to [specific thing], here's a [15s demo / quick link]. We hit [specific ceiling / need].

Would [specific, scoped ask] be possible? Either way, happy to share what we learned.

[Name]
[Short handle / company]
```

For warm outreach (someone you have any tie to), drop the "proof you looked" move and replace with the tie. Tie > observation every time.
