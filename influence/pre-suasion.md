# Pre-Suasion — notes

Cialdini's 2016 follow-up. The short version: *what you draw attention to just before the message shapes how the message is received*. Persuasion starts before the ask.

## Core thesis

Channeled attention creates temporary importance. Whatever you make salient in the reader's mind right before the ask gets over-weighted in their decision. Cialdini calls these **privileged moments** — windows in which the reader is already primed to be receptive to a specific frame, because you've quietly put it in front of them first.

Two mechanisms:

1. **Attention = importance.** Humans reliably assign disproportionate weight to whatever's currently in working memory. A prior cue that's unrelated to the ask in logic but relates in theme will still pull the decision toward the cue.
2. **Associative priming.** Concepts don't activate alone — they spread to associated concepts. Prime "adventure" and readers lean toward risk-taking options even on unrelated questions.

## Privileged moments — engineering them

A privileged moment is any point where the reader's attention is *on a concept that supports your ask*, but before the ask is visible. You can engineer them with:

- **Subject line / hero image / opening line** — the very first thing the reader encounters. This is 80% of the game in digital. Most copywriters treat it as a teaser; treat it instead as pre-suasion.
- **Questions** — asking a question directs the reader's attention exactly where you want it, and does so *voluntarily* (no reactance). "When was the last time your toddler actually surprised you?" primes parent-identity + emotional attention to the kid before any mention of the product.
- **Environmental cues** — images, icons, backgrounds. Cialdini cites an online furniture retailer A/B test: landing page with clouds in the background → visitors weighted comfort features higher; with coins in the background → weighted price higher. Same site, same products.
- **The "because" opener** — Langer 1978 photocopier study: "May I use the copier *because I have to make copies*" — even tautological reason-giving increased compliance from 60% to 93%. The word "because" pre-suades that a reason exists, whether or not it's a good one.
- **Anchoring** — any number shown near the decision shifts the estimate of other numbers toward it. Price displays, "typical" counts, etc.

## How this layers on the 7 principles

The 7 principles are *what* persuades. Pre-suasion is the *when* and the *frame*.

- **Reciprocity** — a pre-suasive opening that makes the reader feel *seen* (a specific observation about their situation) is itself a gift, and sets up the ask.
- **Commitment** — asking a pre-suasive question that the reader answers in their head ("when was the last time your toddler surprised you?") creates a micro-commitment without a click.
- **Social proof** — priming the reader with in-group cues before showing the proof makes the proof feel more relevant.
- **Authority** — leading with a piece of domain insight (not a credential) pre-suades the reader toward taking you as expert, without triggering persuasion-knowledge as a claim of authority would.
- **Liking** — a warm, specific first line primes similarity before the ask.
- **Scarcity** — making the reader think first about what's uniquely theirs or at risk of loss primes them toward the scarcity frame.
- **Unity** — naming the tribe before the ask invites the reader into it first; the ask then feels like an in-group request.

## Advice vs. opinion (a pre-suasion concept with big practical teeth)

Cialdini cites research showing "*can I get your advice?*" puts readers in a **merged-self / partner** frame, whereas "*can I get your opinion / feedback?*" puts them in a **distanced-evaluator** frame. Same underlying ask — wildly different response.

- If you want buy-in, engagement, or a positive response → ask for advice.
- If you want honest critical input → ask for opinion.

For partner outreach (Kling, MiniMax, fellow founders): ask for *advice* on a technical or strategic question first, *then* surface the ask. They're now in collaborator mode.

## Practical pre-suasion moves by channel

### Email
- **Subject line** is the pre-suasion. Pick what state you want the reader in when they open. Curiosity, flattery, warmth, concreteness — one, deliberately.
- **Opening sentence** is the frame. "I've been thinking about [their specific work] because…" pre-suades that this is a thoughtful reader, not a mass-send.

### Landing page
- **Hero** should *not* be the value prop; it should be the frame. What do you want the reader thinking about when they read the value prop below it? Show that.
- **Above-the-fold image** is doing pre-suasion whether you planned it or not. Choose it.

### Product / onboarding
- **The first question you ask a new user** is your strongest pre-suasion lever. Make it a question whose answer is the frame they'll be in for the rest of the flow. For iniminimo: ask the milestone question first, not the email.

### Cold SMS
- No subject line to work with. The first phrase *is* the pre-suasion. Lead with the concrete thing you noticed about them. Never lead with "Hi!" or your name.

### Ads
- The first 1.5 seconds of video or the first word of static copy. Most ads waste this on brand. Use it for the frame.

## What not to do

- Don't pre-suade with something you then contradict. (Opening a parenting app email with "your kid deserves focus" and then selling a screen-time product = detection + reactance.)
- Don't stack primes. One pre-suasive cue does a lot; three read as manipulation.
- Don't use pre-suasion to mask a weak underlying offer. The moment lifts the ask; it doesn't create value.

## Quick test

Before sending anything: cover everything except the first thing the reader sees (subject line, hero, opening). Does that first thing put them in exactly the state of mind you want them in when they see your ask? If not, rewrite *that*, not the body.
